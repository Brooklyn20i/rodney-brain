## Summary
Eric Siu argues that mandating “AI usage” can backfire when teams optimize for activity (outputs) instead of outcomes (revenue, pipeline, conversions). He describes shifting from tool adoption metrics to an accountability system: a “Beat Claude” hiring challenge, weekly AI shipping audits via an agent, office hours, and hackathons. He proposes an AI fluency ladder and claims the biggest marketing ROI comes from high-verifiability loops like CRO and experimentation, not content volume.

## Why It Matters
AI transformations fail when leaders measure adoption instead of measurable business impact. The post offers an operating model: set outcome metrics, build feedback loops, and make capability visible through regular demos and audits. AI ROI improves when work is instrumented, experimentation cycles are short, and roles are redesigned around measurable outcomes rather than tool usage.

## Here's what actually fixed it at Single Grain
The mandate alone was useless. What moved the needle was building accountability into the system:A "Beat Claude" hiring challenge. Every new hire has to prove they can use AI to outperform the tool at their own job. If you can't, you don't get in. This filters for people who are already AI-native, not people who'll say "I love using ChatGPT" in the interview and never touch it again.Friday AI agents. We have an AI agent that checks what the team actually shipped with AI that week. Not "did you use it" but "what measurable outcome did it produce." It shows up in Slack every Friday. You can't hide from it. The agent also flags people who aren't keeping up and surfaces coaching opportunities for them. It's not just about exposure, it's about helping people level up.Weekly AI office hours. We call on people to show what they've built that actually helped a customer. Real work, real outcomes, shared with the whole team. This turns learning into peer pressure. Hackathons. Dedicated time for the team to build something new with AI. The experiments that come out of these end up running in production within weeks.AI Fluency Program. The goal: getting every person on the team to an adaptive level of AI fluency. Not just using AI, but adapting and building with it. We stole the framework from Zapier. It works.The result was predictable: A players went crazy with it. They started building things I never asked for. Our SEO team built a competitive monitoring system on their own. Sales built a deal prep agent that briefs them before every call.C players got exposed. AI didn't replace them. It made it obvious they weren't doing much in the first place.

## This maps to a broader principle: verifiability determines AI ROI.
The closer a marketing function is to a measurable outcome, the better AI performs. The farther away, the more likely you're paying for expensive noise.CRO is the clearest example. You change a headline, measure the conversion rate, see if it went up. Binary. Fast feedback loop. AI can run 50 variants in the time a human tests 2. The output is verifiable within days, not months.We run AI agents on conversion experiments at Single Grain. The agent proposes changes, measures results, and kills losers automatically. No meetings. No "let's discuss the data next sprint." The system has a feedback loop measured in hours, not quarters.That's why CRO is the single highest-ROI use case for AI in marketing right now. Not content generation. Not social posting. Conversion optimization.
